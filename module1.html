Understanding Child Rights

This section defines who is a child and discusses the child’s rights as prescribed by various national and international laws and guidelines. Learners will be equipped with the knowledge of the basic rights for children as enshrined in the UN Charter for Child Rights and Nigeria’s Child's Rights Act (CRA), 2003 as well as the Ondo State Child’s Rights Law (ODCRL) 2007.

Objectives

  • To introduce the idea of child and childhood.

  • To familiarize Learners with basic child rights.

  • To make the Learners understand the importance of child rights.

Who is a Child and What is Childhood?

The UN Convention on the Rights of the Child United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child (UNCRC) defines “any person under the age of 18 years as a child”. Nigeria is a party to this and ratified it in the year 1999. So, by definition, globally and in Nigeria, any person under the age of 18 years is considered a child and the Nigerian Constitution accords certain special rights to children as citizens of the country.

Childhood, as defined by UNICEF, and as distinctly separated from adulthood “is the time for children to be in school and at play to grow strong and confident with the love and encouragement of their family and an extended community of caring adults,” The UNCRC defines childhood as a separate space from adulthood and recognises that what is appropriate for an adult may not be suitable for a child. Further, it also recognises children as holders of their own rights and not passive recipients of charity.

What are Child Rights?

Child’s rights law started with the United Nations convention on the rights of children in 1989, The African Union (formerly Organisation of African Unity) domesticated the convention to be in conformity with African values in 1990. Nigeria domesticated it to become the Nigeria Child’s Rights Act in 2003, while Ondo state is among the 30 states that have domesticated it to be in conformity with the culture and norm of the people in 2007. Find below, key references to the child and child’s rights in the sited documents:

The United Nation Convention on the Rights of Children (CRA), 1989

  • Article 2(1) States Parties shall respect and ensure the rights set forth in the present Convention to each child within their jurisdiction without discrimination of any kind, irrespective of the child's or his or her parent's or legal guardian's race, color, sex, language, religion, political or other opinion, national, ethnic, or social origin, property, disability, birth, or other status.

Africa Charter on the rights and welfare of the child ACRWC (African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of a Child), 1990

  • Article 2: Every child shall be entitled to the enjoyment of the rights and freedoms recognised and guaranteed in this Charter irrespective of the child’s or his/her parents’ or legal guardians’ race, ethnic group, colour, sex, language, religion, political or other opinion, national and social origin, fortune, birth, or other status.

Nigeria's Constitution of 1999 (with Amendments through 2011)

  • Chapter IV provides for the fundamental human (including children) rights, such as right to life, respect for dignity of a person, personal liberty, freedom of thought and expression.

Child's Rights Act (CRA),2003

  • Section 3-18 highlights the rights of the child which includes rights to survival and development, right to name, private life, dignity, parental care, freedom of thought, freedom of movement, freedom from discrimination.

Ondo State Childs Rights Law (ODCRL) 2007

  • Part 1, section 1 states that in every action concerning a child, whether undertaken by an individual, public, or private body, institutions or service, court of law, or administrative or legislative authority, the best interest of the child shall be the primary consideration.

What are Childs Rights?

Child’s Rights are minimum entitlements and freedoms that should be afforded to all persons below the age of 18, regardless of race, colour, gender language, religion, opinions, origins, wealth, birth-status, or disability status and therefore apply to all children everywhere.

Do You Know the 15 Basic Child’s Rights?

  • Right to Survival and Development.

  • Right to Name.

  • Freedom of Association and Peaceful Assembly.

  • Freedom of Thought, Conscience and Religion.

  • Right to Private and Family Life.

  • Right to Freedom of Movement.

  • Right to Freedom from Discrimination.

  • Right to Dignity of the Child.

  • Right to Leisure, Recreation and Cultural Activities.

  • Right to Health and Heal Services.

  • Right to Parental Care, Protection and Maintenance.

  • Right of Child to Free, Compulsory and Universal Primary Education, etc.

  • Right of Child in Need of Special Protection Measure.

  • Right of the Unborn Child to Protection Against Harm, etc.

  • Contractual Rights of Child.

Conclusion

The convention, charter, acts, and law identified the special reason to protect the rights of all children. These rights are universal and must be enforced by all nation signatory to the UNCRC. The rights are to ensure all children grow to become the right adult.

Parent’s and Guardian’s Affirmation

I am to protect the rights of every child around me no matter the color, sex, language, religion, political or other opinion, national, ethnic, or social origin, property, disability, birth, or other status.

Lessons Learnt

Child’s rights are human rights. Adults, institutions, and everyone is responsible for ensuring the child's rights are protected.
