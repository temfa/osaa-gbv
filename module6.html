Reporting child protection Issues

In the earlier modules, you learnt about different types of child abuse. We also discussed the myths about child abuse. It is also identified that prompt reporting of child abuse is very important. If you see anything that can harm a child, say something. In this module you will learn about what, when, and where to report.

What to report

There are signs to lookout for in a child to identify suspected cases of child abuse. For example.

  • A mood swing in a child might be because of abuse.

  • A child with several scars on the body, who also does not want to talk or could not talk about the scars may be a victim of child abuse.

  • When discussing generally about a person or place, and the child intentionally avoids talking about the person or place freely as others, such child may be a victim of child abuse.

  • A child who suddenly withdraws from others, and activities, may be a victim of child abuse.

  • A once brilliant child whose academic performance dropped may be having unresolved issues in mind which could be caused by abuse in the house, school or within the community.

  • When a child regularly talks about adult-related issues, attempting, or practicing such on other children, especially sexual acts, or physical attack, may be a sign of an abused child.

  • A child who ran away from home may be a victim of abuse.

When to report

Reporting suspected child abuse is to be done on time. The life of a child may be at stake, therefore every minute counts. For instance, a case of rape requires prompt medical attention and report which will be used in prosecution of the suspect in the courts. A grown-up child may also need pregnancy prevention treatment administered within 72 hours (about 3 days). Please report a suspected case of child abuse promptly.

Where to report

There are several channels and places for reporting cases of child abuse. This can be done through:

  • Telephone calls

  • Emails

  • Social media

  • Walk in

  • Letter writing or petition.

Places to report

  • Law enforcement agencies.

  • Social welfare institutions.

  • Religious institutions such as churches and mosques.

  • NGO (Non-Governmental Organisations) focused on children welfare.

Who to report to

Anyone in a position to provide immediate or long-term assistance, rescue or care for a child could be the person to report to. Do not broadcast to people who cannot address the situation. Who to report to includes.

  • Police, or other law enforcement agencies, especially those working with children.

  • Community leaders.

  • School heads/managements.

  • A respected and trusted adult.

  • A social welfare officer or social worker with no history of child abuse.

Actions to Take When a Child Shares a Protection Issue

  • Praise the child for seeking help.

  • Be attentive and open minded.

  • Use open-ended questions or statements.

  • Do not apportion blame, rather empathize with the child.

  • Introduce the next line of action.

Prohibited Actions When a Child Shares a Protection Issue

  • Outright expression of views and feelings about the incident.

  • Drilling the child to extract more details of the incident.

  • Do not show or express disbelief.

  • Do not make promises beyond getting help.
